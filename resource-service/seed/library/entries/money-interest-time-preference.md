## Interest is the price of time

Offered $1,000 today or $1,000 in five years, everyone takes it today. A bird in the hand is worth more; you could use, enjoy, or invest the money now, and the future is uncertain. This near-universal preference for the present over the future is called **time preference**.

**Interest** is the price that bridges that gap. To persuade you to part with money *now* and wait, a borrower must offer you *more* later. The interest rate is, at root, the market price of giving up present goods for future ones.

> **Framing note.** The "interest = time preference" account is central to the Austrian school (Mises, Rothbard). Other traditions emphasize the supply and demand for loanable funds, liquidity preference (Keynes), or central-bank policy. These aren't wholly contradictory — they describe different layers — but this entry leans on the time-preference view, hence its *medium* confidence rating. Hold it as a powerful model, not settled fact.

## Why the interest rate is a vital signal

In an unhampered market, the interest rate coordinates **savers** and **investors** across time:

- When people save more (lower time preference — they're willing to wait), the supply of lendable funds rises and rates **fall**. Lower rates signal: "resources are available for long-term projects; build."
- When people save less (higher time preference — they want to consume now), rates **rise**, signaling: "capital is scarce; don't overextend."

So the interest rate is a giant, decentralized signal about how much society is willing to defer consumption. Entrepreneurs read it to decide which long-term projects are worth starting.

## What happens when the signal is faked

Now recall from *Banking & How Money Is Created* that the central bank **sets** a key interest rate, and that cheap credit expands the money supply. When the central bank pushes rates **below** the level real savings would have produced, it sends a false signal:

- Borrowing looks cheap, so businesses launch long-term projects **as if** abundant savings existed to complete them — but the savings aren't really there.
- Savers, earning little, are pushed toward riskier assets in search of yield, inflating asset prices (recall the Cantillon effect — asset owners are near the spigot).
- Eventually the mismatch surfaces: projects that looked profitable at fake-low rates aren't, and a cluster of them fail together.

This is the core of the **Austrian business cycle theory**: artificially suppressed interest rates cause *malinvestment* (resources poured into the wrong long-term projects), an unsustainable boom, and then a corrective bust. *This theory is influential but contested among economists; treat it as one compelling explanation, not consensus.*

## What to take away

- Interest is fundamentally the **price of time**, rooted in human time preference.
- A market interest rate is a **coordination signal** between savers and investors.
- When that rate is suppressed by policy, it can misallocate capital and inflate asset bubbles — a major reason "low rates forever" has side effects.
- Lowering your *own* time preference — saving, delaying gratification, thinking long — is the personal counterpart to all this, and arguably the highest-leverage financial habit.
