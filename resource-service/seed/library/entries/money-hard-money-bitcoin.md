## "Hard" vs. "easy" money

Recall **salability across time** and the **stock-to-flow** ratio from *Origins & Properties*. **Hard money** is money whose supply is hard to increase — a high stock-to-flow, so new production can't dilute existing holders. **Easy money** is the opposite: its supply can be expanded cheaply, so it fails as a long-term store of value.

History is full of monies that were hard *until they weren't*: seashells and glass beads worked until mass production flooded them; even gold-backed currencies softened once governments could issue more paper than they held in gold (see *From Gold to Fiat*). The pattern is consistent — **the moment a money's supply can be cheaply expanded, its savers lose.**

## Why gold won for millennia

Gold became the dominant money because it scored highest on the hard-money test: its above-ground stock is enormous relative to the small annual mining flow, so no one could meaningfully inflate it. It's also durable, divisible, fungible, and verifiable. Its weaknesses were **portability** and **verifiability at a distance** — you can't email gold, and validating it across the world is hard. That weakness is precisely what let governments centralize it into vaults and issue redeemable paper on top — paper they could later print beyond the gold backing.

## What Bitcoin proposes

Bitcoin (introduced in Satoshi Nakamoto's 2008 whitepaper, in the sources) is an attempt to build hard money that is *also* portable and verifiable across any distance, without a trusted central issuer. Its key monetary properties:

- **Fixed supply.** The protocol caps the total at **21 million** coins. New supply is issued on a fixed schedule that **halves roughly every four years** (the "halving"), trending toward zero. Its scarcity is enforced by code and consensus, not by the restraint of an institution.
- **Decentralized issuance.** No central party can expand the supply or reverse the schedule. There is no "gold window" to close (see *From Gold to Fiat*) — and so, in principle, no Cantillon-style discretionary printing.
- **Self-custody.** You can hold it yourself with a private key, and verify the entire money supply with your own software. This is digital scarcity you can personally audit — historically unprecedented.
- **Portable & verifiable.** It moves over the internet and is cryptographically verifiable anywhere, addressing gold's two great weaknesses.

## The honest trade-offs

This wing rates this entry *medium* confidence on purpose. Hard money is a sound principle; **Bitcoin specifically** is a young, contested, and volatile experiment. An honest ledger:

- **Volatility.** Over short horizons Bitcoin's price swings violently — a poor *unit of account* and a nerve-wracking *store of value* in the short run, whatever its long-run thesis.
- **Scaling & usability.** The base layer processes limited transactions; everyday payments rely on additional layers and infrastructure that are still maturing.
- **Energy.** Its proof-of-work security consumes significant electricity. Defenders argue this is the cost of decentralized, un-inflatable money (and increasingly uses stranded/renewable energy); critics see waste. Both sides have real points.
- **Custody risk.** "Be your own bank" means *you* carry the risk: lose your keys and the money is gone, with no helpline. Sovereignty and responsibility are the same coin.
- **It is not a guaranteed outcome.** Adoption, regulation, and technical risk are all open questions. Hard money is a strong *idea*; whether Bitcoin durably fulfills it is not settled.

## What to take away

- The thread running through this whole wing is **resistance to supply expansion** — that, more than anything, is what makes money a good store of value.
- Gold embodied that physically; fiat deliberately abandoned it (1971); Bitcoin attempts to restore it digitally and verifiably.
