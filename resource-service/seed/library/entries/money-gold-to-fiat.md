## What "backed by gold" meant

Under a **gold standard**, paper money was a *claim* on a fixed amount of metal. A dollar was, in effect, a receipt you could redeem for gold. This put a hard leash on governments: you couldn't issue more receipts than you could (eventually) honor in gold, so you couldn't inflate the money supply at will. The discipline was the point — and the constraint that politicians in a crisis most want to escape.

## Bretton Woods (1944): the dollar becomes the anchor

After World War II, 44 nations met at Bretton Woods and built a new system:

- The **U.S. dollar** was pegged to gold at **$35 per ounce**.
- **Other currencies** were pegged to the dollar.
- The dollar became the world's reserve currency — as good as gold, because it was redeemable for gold by foreign governments.

This worked while the U.S. held enough gold to back the dollars circulating abroad. But the U.S. ran deficits and printed dollars (funding domestic programs and the Vietnam War) faster than its gold reserves grew. By the late 1960s, foreign dollars vastly outnumbered the gold available to redeem them. France and others began demanding gold for their dollars — a slow-motion bank run on Fort Knox.

## August 15, 1971: the gold window closes

Facing a gold drain and rising inflation, President Nixon and his advisers met secretly at Camp David. On the evening of **August 15, 1971**, Nixon announced that the U.S. would **"temporarily" suspend the convertibility of dollars into gold**, alongside wage and price controls.

The suspension was never reversed. Attempts to patch the system (the Smithsonian Agreement) failed, and by 1973 the major currencies floated freely. The dollar — and with it the entire global monetary system — became **fiat**: money that is legal tender by government decree, backed by trust and the power to tax, not by any fixed commodity.

## Why this is the hinge of modern finance

Once money no longer had to be redeemable for a scarce metal, the supply of money became a matter of **policy**, not geology. That single change underlies almost everything that follows in this wing:

- Central banks can now expand the money supply with no hard ceiling.
- Sustained, ongoing inflation became the norm rather than the exception.
- Governments can finance deficits by creating money (directly or via the banking system).
- The *store-of-value* function of money — the one most easily broken — was handed over to institutions with a standing incentive and ability to expand the supply.

None of this says fiat is a conspiracy or that it can't "work." It says the **constraint changed**, and understanding *which* constraint changed is the key to reading everything from inflation to interest rates to the appeal of hard money.
