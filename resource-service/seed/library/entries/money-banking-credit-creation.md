## The story most people carry around (and why it's wrong)

The textbook picture goes: savers deposit money, the bank keeps a fraction in reserve, and lends the rest out; that loan gets re-deposited, lent again, and so on — the "money multiplier." It's intuitive. It's also, as a description of how banks actually operate, **backwards**.

The Bank of England put it bluntly in its 2014 paper *Money creation in the modern economy*: banks do **not** simply lend out deposits that savers place with them, and they do **not** "multiply up" central bank money. Instead —

## What really happens: loans create deposits

When a bank makes you a loan, it does **not** hand you someone else's savings. It simultaneously writes two new entries on its own balance sheet:

- An **asset**: your obligation to repay the loan.
- A **liability**: a brand-new deposit in your account, which you can now spend.

![How a bank loan creates new money](/library-assets/money/money-creation.svg)

That deposit is **new money** that did not exist a moment earlier — created with a keystroke. Most of the money in a modern economy (the cash in everyone's bank accounts) was created exactly this way, by commercial banks extending credit. When you repay the loan, that money is **destroyed** — the deposit and the loan cancel out. Money is continuously created by lending and extinguished by repayment.

## So what *actually* limits money creation?

If banks can conjure deposits, why don't they create infinite money? Because they face real constraints — just not the "vault of reserves" one:

- **Profitability and risk** — each loan must be expected to be repaid; bad loans are losses.
- **Capital requirements** — banks must hold enough loss-absorbing capital against their assets (this, not reserves, is the binding regulatory limit).
- **Competition and demand** — banks need creditworthy borrowers who *want* to borrow.
- **Monetary policy** — the central bank sets the **interest rate**, which raises or lowers the price of borrowing and so throttles how much new credit (and money) is demanded. This is the main lever, not a quantity cap on reserves.

Note: the old "fractional reserve requirement" has faded as a constraint — for example, the U.S. Federal Reserve reduced reserve requirement ratios to **zero** in March 2020. Banks are limited by capital and policy, not by a reserve ratio.

## Why this matters for you

- **Inflation has a private engine, too.** Money isn't only printed by the central bank; it's created whenever the banking system expands credit. Booms of cheap credit *are* expansions of the money supply.
- **Interest rates are the throttle.** Lowering rates encourages borrowing, which creates money and fuels asset prices; raising them does the reverse. This is why rate decisions move everything.
- **The system is debt-based.** In a fiat system, most money exists *because* someone is in debt. Pay off all debt and you'd extinguish most of the money supply — a strange and important property worth sitting with.

Once you see that loans create deposits, the modern economy's reflexes — credit booms, asset bubbles, the obsession with interest rates — stop looking mysterious.
