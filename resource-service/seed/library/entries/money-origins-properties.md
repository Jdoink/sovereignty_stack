## The problem money solves

Direct barter has a crippling flaw: the **double coincidence of wants**. To trade, I must find someone who has what I want *and* wants what I have, right now. A baker who wants shoes must find a cobbler who happens to want bread. As the number of goods grows, the number of possible pairings explodes and trade grinds down.

A society escapes this by converging on one good that *everyone* will accept — not because they want it directly, but because they know others will take it later. The baker sells bread for that good, confident the cobbler (and everyone else) will take it too. That good becomes **money**. In Carl Menger's classic account, money is not invented by decree; it emerges from individuals each reaching for the *most marketable* good they can get.

> **An honest caveat.** This "barter → money" story (Menger, and Rothbard below) is the classical economic account. Some anthropologists (notably David Graeber) argue that credit and IOU systems often predated coin-based trade, and that pure barter economies were rare. The *functions* of money below hold regardless of which origin story is right.

## The three functions

Whatever its origin, money does three jobs:

- **Medium of exchange** — breaks the barter deadlock.
- **Store of value** — lets you hold the fruits of today's work to spend later.
- **Unit of account** — a common ruler for pricing everything.

The store-of-value job is the one most easily broken — and the one this whole wing keeps coming back to.

## The properties that make good money

Over history, the goods that won as money shared a recognizable set of traits:

![The six properties of good money](/library-assets/money/properties.svg)

- **Scarce** — supply can't be easily expanded (this is the big one).
- **Durable** — doesn't rot or corrode.
- **Divisible** — splits into small units for small trades.
- **Portable** — high value packs into little space.
- **Fungible** — one unit is interchangeable with another.
- **Verifiable** — hard to counterfeit, easy to authenticate.

## The property that decides everything: salability across time

Of all these, the deepest is **salability across time** — will the good hold its value into the future? That depends almost entirely on whether its supply can be inflated.

A useful lens is the **stock-to-flow** ratio: the existing *stock* of a good divided by the new *flow* produced each year. Gold's stock is enormous relative to the trickle of new mining, so no one can flood the market and debase holders — which is why gold served as money for thousands of years. Cattle, seashells, beads, and copper all worked as money until someone found a way to produce far more of them, at which point they failed as a store of value.

This is the core insight to carry forward: **money is only as good as its resistance to having its supply expanded.** Hold onto it as you read about fiat (where supply is expanded deliberately) and Bitcoin (where supply is capped by code).
