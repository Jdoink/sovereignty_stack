## Two meanings of "inflation"

The word gets used two ways, and conflating them causes confusion:

- **Monetary inflation** — the *quantity of money* expands. (Measured by aggregates like **M2** — see the FRED series in the sources.)
- **Price inflation** — the *general price level* rises, so each unit of money buys less. (Measured by indexes like the **CPI**.)

The first tends to drive the second, but not mechanically or instantly. A simple lens is the **equation of exchange**, MV = PQ: money supply × how fast it circulates = price level × real output. If money (M) grows faster than the output of goods (Q), and velocity (V) doesn't collapse, prices (P) rise. *Caveat: this is an accounting identity used as a model; velocity is not constant and the relationship is loose in the short run. Treat it as a way to organize thinking, not a precise predictor.*

The headline point survives all the nuance: **print money faster than the economy produces real goods, and prices rise.** Inflation is, in Milton Friedman's famous (and debated) phrasing, "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."

## Inflation is a hidden tax on savers

Because new money doesn't make the society's pile of real goods any bigger, expanding the money supply doesn't create wealth — it **redistributes** it. Every new unit dilutes the purchasing power of the units you already hold, exactly like printing extra tickets at the school fair. You can work hard, save diligently, do everything right, and still watch your stored value erode. That erosion is a transfer from savers to whoever issued and first spent the new money — which is why inflation is sometimes called a tax that was never voted on.

You can feel the scale of it: by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics' own CPI calculator (in the sources), a dollar buys a small fraction of what it did decades ago. The dollar is *designed* to lose value over time.

## The Cantillon effect: first in line wins

Here's the part most discussions miss. New money does **not** rain down on everyone equally and simultaneously. It enters the economy at **specific points** — through the banking system, government spending, and asset purchases — and ripples outward.

Richard Cantillon (1680s–1734) noticed the consequence:

- **Those who receive the new money first** — banks, governments, large borrowers, owners of assets that get bid up first — spend it **before** prices have risen. They buy at the old, lower prices.
- **Those who receive it last** — wage earners, savers, people on fixed incomes — find that by the time the money reaches them, prices have *already* risen. They buy at the new, higher prices.

So inflation isn't a neutral, uniform shrinkage. It's a **systematic transfer** from the end of the line to the front of it. This is the **Cantillon effect**, and it quietly explains a great deal about who gets richer when money is loose: asset owners ahead of wage earners, the financially connected ahead of the disconnected.

## What to take away

- "Inflation" can mean expanding money *or* rising prices; watch which one someone means.
- Expanding the money supply redistributes wealth; it doesn't create it.
- The redistribution favors whoever is **closest to the money spigot** — the Cantillon effect.
- This is the mechanical reason a sound, supply-capped money matters to anyone who saves.
