Understanding money intellectually is one thing; *feeling* it in your own life is another. These projects build that instinct. Do them in order.

## Project 1 — Measure your own debasement (½ day)

Use the **BLS CPI Inflation Calculator** (in sources) to answer: what would the salary you (or a parent) earned in the year you were born be worth today? Then look up the **M2 money supply** on FRED and chart its growth over the same period.

**Goal:** see, in your own numbers, the gap between nominal and real — and notice how closely money-supply growth tracks the erosion of purchasing power. Write the two numbers in a new Library entry of your own.

## Project 2 — Build a personal price basket (ongoing)

Pick 8–10 things you actually buy (rent, a dozen eggs, gas, a streaming subscription, a favorite meal). Record today's prices. Revisit every 6 months.

**Goal:** build your *own* inflation index. Official CPI is an average across a basket you may not share; your lived inflation can differ sharply. This is the single best antidote to "inflation is only 3%" when your rent says otherwise.

## Project 3 — Hold value yourself (a weekend)

Choose one and actually do it (small amounts — this is a learning exercise, not financial advice):
- Buy a small amount of **physical gold or silver** from a reputable dealer and store it. Notice the premiums, verification, and storage friction — gold's real-world weaknesses.
- Or set up **self-custody of a small amount of Bitcoin**: generate keys, write down a seed phrase, send and receive a tiny transaction, and verify it on a block explorer.

**Goal:** experience *being your own bank* — both the sovereignty and the responsibility. The friction you feel is the lesson.

## Project 4 — Teach it back (½ day)

Explain the Cantillon effect, or why "banks lend out deposits" is wrong, to someone with no background — or write it as a fresh ELI5 entry in your Library.

**Goal:** the Feynman test. If you can't explain it simply, you don't yet understand it. Teaching exposes the gaps.

## Read the primary texts (in this order)

Don't just read *about* these ideas — read the originals (all free or widely available; full links in **Resources**):

1. **Henry Hazlitt, *Economics in One Lesson*** — the mental discipline of seeing the unseen, second-order effects. Start here.
2. **Murray Rothbard, *What Has Government Done to Our Money?*** — short, sharp history of money's decline. (Free PDF.)
3. **Saifedean Ammous, *The Bitcoin Standard*** — the modern hard-money thesis, history through to Bitcoin.
4. **Satoshi Nakamoto, *Bitcoin Whitepaper*** — 9 pages; read the source, not the hot takes.

For balance, seek out a strong **critique** of each (e.g., mainstream/Keynesian responses, and skeptical takes on Bitcoin). Mastery means being able to argue *both* sides honestly.

## You've mastered this wing when…

- You can explain where money comes from, what changed in 1971, and how banks create it — without notes.
- You track your own purchasing power and aren't fooled by a headline inflation number.
- You've personally held value outside the banking system, and understand the trade-offs first-hand.
- You can steelman both the hard-money case *and* its strongest critics.
