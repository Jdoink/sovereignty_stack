## The school-fair ticket

Imagine your school runs a fair. Instead of swapping a cupcake for a turn on the trampoline (hard — maybe the trampoline kid doesn't want cupcakes), everyone trades **tickets**. You earn tickets by doing things people value and spend them on things you want.

A ticket isn't worth anything by itself — you can't eat it. It's worth something because **everyone agrees** it can be exchanged for real stuff, *later*, with *someone else*. Money is that ticket, scaled up to a whole society.

So money is really three things at once:

- **A way to trade** without needing the other person to want exactly what you have (a *medium of exchange*).
- **A scoreboard** of value you've created but haven't spent yet (a *store of value*).
- **A common ruler** for pricing wildly different things — an hour of work, a loaf of bread, a house (a *unit of account*).

## The one thing to watch

Here's the part that decides whether your tickets stay valuable: **how many exist.**

If the fair organizer can secretly print extra tickets for themselves, every ticket you saved buys a little less. You did nothing wrong, yet your savings quietly shrank — and whoever printed the new tickets got to spend them first, at the old prices. That quiet shrinking is **inflation**, and who gets to print is the most important question in economics.

That's the whole game in miniature. Good money is money whose supply *can't* be easily inflated — so the value you store today is still there tomorrow.
