A shelf chosen for **signal and longevity**. Where a free, archivable copy exists, that's the link given. Read across traditions — the goal is fluency, not allegiance.

## Watch / listen first

- **Ray Dalio — *How the Economic Machine Works*** (30 min). The single best on-ramp to credit, debt cycles, and the machine's shape.

## Primary documents (read the source, not the summary)

- **Bank of England — *Money creation in the modern economy* (2014)**. A central bank explaining, in its own words, that loans create deposits. The myth-buster.
- **Federal Reserve History — *Nixon Ends Convertibility* (1971)**. The hinge of modern money, from the Fed's own historians.
- **Satoshi Nakamoto — *Bitcoin Whitepaper* (2008)**. Nine pages that launched digital scarcity.

## Free books

- **Henry Hazlitt — *Economics in One Lesson*** (free at Mises). The discipline of second-order thinking. Best first book.
- **Murray Rothbard — *What Has Government Done to Our Money?*** (free PDF). Short history of money's decline; Austrian lens.
- **Saifedean Ammous — *The Bitcoin Standard*** (Wiley, 2018). Hard-money history through to Bitcoin.

## Live data (verify reality yourself)

- **FRED — M2 Money Stock**. Watch the money supply in real time.
- **BLS — CPI Inflation Calculator**. Convert any past dollar amount to today's purchasing power.

## A note on balance

This shelf leans toward the **Austrian / sound-money** tradition, because that lens makes the *store-of-value* problem and the case for hard money especially clear — and because it's under-represented in mainstream coverage. But it is **one** tradition. To be genuinely educated, deliberately read its critics too: Keynesian and Modern Monetary Theory perspectives on government deficits and money, and rigorous skeptics of Bitcoin. Add the best of those to this wing as you find them — a Library that only argues one side isn't a library, it's a pamphlet.
